The problem

A system built on sponsorships and luck

Unlike countries such as the United Kingdom and China, the U.S. government provides no direct funding to its Olympic athletes. Athletes rely on private sponsorships, grants, or personal savings. This system rewards visibility over talent only the most marketable and popular athletes attract major deals, while many others receive nothing.

A 2024 congressional report surveyed roughly 1,000 American athletes in the Olympic and Paralympic pipeline and found that 26.5% reported individual incomes under $15,000 a year while average annual competition costs run about $12,000.
Commission on the State of U.S. Olympics & Paralympics, 2024. The Wall Street Journal

Meanwhile, the USOPC generated $497 million in revenue in 2024, its highest ever, while its CEO earned $1.25 million in total compensation. The gap between company wealth and athlete income reflect a contradiction for the U.S. Olympic sports.

Why it's an inequality issue

Four core problems

⏱

Full time commitment

Olympic training is to many a full time job, often 20–35+ hours per week. Leaving little room for them to get an actual job.

📉

Unstable income

Sponsorships favor visibility. Medal bonuses ($37,500 gold / $22,500 silver / $15,000 bronze) reach a very small percentage of athletes who compete.

🎓

Long term sacrifice

Athletes often retire as young as 25–30 with little to no savings, having spent their prime earning years training instead of building careers.

🌍

Global disadvantage

U.S. athletes compete against rivals from countries with direct government stipends. Great Britain, for example, provides athletes about $36,000 per year to train.

A 2025 change

The Stevens Awards a private solution to a public problem

Announced March 2025: Entrepreneur Ross Stevens donated $100 million to the USOPC, its largest-ever single gift. Starting with the 2026 Milan-Cortina Winter Games, every qualifying U.S. Olympic and Paralympic athlete earning under $1M/year will receive $200,000 in deferred financial benefits per Games. $100K becomes available at age 45 (or 20 years after competing); $100K is a guaranteed death benefit for the athlete's family. Awards stack across multiple Games.

This is a meaningful step, but it is private philanthropy, not government policy, and the benefits are deferred rather than immediate. Athletes still face financial hardship during their active careers. The need for structural reform remains.

A real training day

One day in the life of an Olympic rower

Kristin Hedstrom competed at the 2012 London Olympics and won silver at the 2013 World Championships in the lightweight double. She kept a detailed training log every single day for nearly 15 years. The entry below is from Wednesday, October 9, 2013, ten days before a major race. Read the full post on her website →

5:30 AM -> Wake up
Daily weigh-in first thing (lightweight rowers must average under 57 kg / 125.7 lbs, with no individual over 59 kg). She blends a banana-almond butter protein shake, checks wind and tides on her phone, and bikes 2 miles to the boathouse in the dark.
6:00 AM -> Warm-up & launch
A 12-minute warm-up jog in the dark with a flashlight. Seven-exercise dynamic warm-up in the boat bay. On the water by 6:30 AM for an all-out 4km race-pace scrimmage against teammates.
7:00–8:00 AM -> Race + cooldown
An 18-minute racing piece into the wind and against the tide. Then 30 minutes of cooldown rowing. Total morning mileage: 14.5km on the water.
8:00–9:30 AM -> Run + recovery
A 53-minute run with a teammate immediately after rowing. Back to the boathouse to stretch and eat, on the locker room floor, while most teammates have already left for their jobs.
Afternoon -> Second full session
Another complete training block in the afternoon. This is not a special occasion, this is a normal Wednesday.
"Training at that level requires two to three practices a day and pushing your body's limits of strength and endurance. Layer days on days and weeks on weeks of that and you're not exactly bounding up every set of stairs you see."
Kristin Hedstrom, 2012 Olympian and 2013 World Championship silver medalist
By the numbers

The scale of Olympic training

📅

20–35+ hours per week

Simone Biles trained about 32 hours per week. Endurance athletes average 13–17 hours at minimum. This is a full-time job.

🏊

Twice-daily sessions

Olympic swimmers practice twice a day, 5–6 hours in the pool, swimming 12–14 km daily, not counting gym, nutrition, and recovery work.

📆

4–8 years to make a team

It is common for Olympic athletes to spend four to eight years in intensive training before making an Olympic team, often beginning as children.

😴

8–10 hours of sleep required

Recovery is training. Elite athletes prioritize 8–10 hours of sleep per night alongside strict nutrition, physical therapy, and mental conditioning.

The financial reality

Training this hard, and atlethes still struggle

Because Olympic training is a full time job, athletes have almost no time for traditional employment. Without government support, many must find ways to earn income on top of their training load. Financial stability becomes a competitive advantage in itself.

Cyclist Kristen Faulkner, one of the biggest success stories of the 2024 Olympics, had to respond to work emails at 6am and attend Zoom meetings until midnight during a 7-day stage race in France just to maintain an income while training. "At times I did not have time to shower until 11pm or eat anything more than a baguette and protein shake for dinner."
Kristen Faulkner's personal blog, reported by Sherwood News, 2024
